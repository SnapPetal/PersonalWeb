America 250 · A personal reflection

Religious freedom is precious because Christ is worthy.

As the United States marks 250 years in 2026, I am thankful for a country where faith is not assigned by government. But this reflection begins with Jesus Christ, who alone saves, reconciles sinners to God, and makes the heart free.

A personal reflection

Liberty of conscience is a public trust.

Religious freedom is more than permission to attend a service. It protects the space where a person can seek God, speak honestly, raise a family, serve neighbors, and live according to conscience without government coercion.

America has not always honored that principle perfectly. Still, the First Amendment remains a remarkable public commitment: civil authority should not establish a national church, and it should not prohibit the free exercise of religion. That restraint matters because worship belongs first to God, not to government.

My hope for America at 250 is simple: that we would preserve liberty of conscience for people with whom we agree and people with whom we disagree, and that Christians would use that freedom to serve with humility, courage, and love.

The freedom that matters most

Civic freedom is precious. Christ is greater.

Religious freedom is a precious civic gift, but it is not the deepest freedom a person needs. The greatest freedom is found in Jesus Christ, who saves sinners by grace through faith, reconciles us to God, and calls us to walk in love and truth.

America can protect the right to worship, speak, gather, and live according to conscience. Only Christ can make the heart free.

A prayer for America

May freedom become faithful service.

Lord, teach us to receive liberty with gratitude and use it with humility. Guard the freedom of conscience in our country, strengthen families and churches, and help us serve our neighbors with courage, truth, and love.

May America's next 250 years be marked not only by liberty preserved, but by lives transformed through faith, truth, and love.
